About the Brain Observatory Data

The Brain Observatory is home to data that comes from visual regions of mouse cortex. There are two way of accessing the data. You can either search by Experiment or by Cell. So how do you decide which method of search to use?

Use the Experiment search if you would like to find neuronal-population responses to stimuli.

Use the Cell search if you would like to find single-neuron responses to stimuli.

An in depth explanation of how to do each of these searches is found under the Tutorials section. For now, let's get you oriented with our experimentation techniques so you understand what our data means

Overview of the Experiments

The data we gathered comes from a collection of experiments. Each experiment follows the same experimental design—a transgenic mouse is stimulated with three synthetic stimuli and two natural stimuli. The key difference between experiments is that the following variables differ:
  • the transgenic mouse line
  • the area of the visual cortex
  • depth at which the cells are imaged in the mouse visual cortex


  • Having transgenic mice allow us to observe fluorescence in cells of different cortical layers.
  • There are six areas of the visual cortex, so we want to see the unique response of neurons in each region
  • observing the activtiy of cells at different depths creates a more distinct picture of how visual info is processed


These differences allow us to paint a larger picture for how neuronal physiology under different circumstances. Here is a rundown of the experimental procedure (look at white papers).

Experiment Design

An experiment is composed of three, one-hour long two-photon imaging calcium sessions on an awake mouse presented with a series of visual stimuli. An Experiment is the unique combination of one mouse, one imaging depth (e.g. 175 um from surface of cortex), and one visual area. Each experiment includes three imaging sessions as illustrated below.
